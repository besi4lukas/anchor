# Crisis Resources and Safety Planning

If you or someone you know is in immediate danger, call 911 or your local emergency number. For mental health crises that are not immediately life-threatening, several confidential services are available around the clock, every day of the year.

## 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline

The 988 Suicide and Crisis Lifeline provides free, confidential support for people in suicidal crisis or emotional distress. You can reach it by calling or texting 988 from any phone in the United States. The service is available twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, three hundred sixty-five days a year.

When you call 988, you will be connected to a trained crisis counselor at one of over two hundred local call centers across the country. The counselor will listen to your situation, help you work through what you are feeling, and connect you with local resources if needed. You do not need to be suicidal to call — the Lifeline also serves people experiencing emotional distress, substance abuse crises, or concern about a loved one.

Specialized services are available through the Lifeline. Veterans and service members can press 1 after dialing 988 to reach the Veterans Crisis Line. A Spanish-language line is available by pressing 2. Deaf and hard of hearing individuals can use their preferred relay service to connect to 988.

## Crisis Text Line

If talking on the phone feels too difficult, the Crisis Text Line provides the same kind of support through text messaging. Text HOME to 741741 to connect with a trained crisis counselor. The service is free, confidential, and available around the clock.

After texting HOME, you will receive an automated response while a counselor is assigned to you, usually within five minutes. The counselor will introduce themselves and begin the conversation at your pace. Texting can feel more manageable than a phone call because it gives you time to think about what you want to say and provides a written record you can refer back to.

The Crisis Text Line serves anyone in any type of crisis, including anxiety, depression, suicidal thoughts, abuse, self-harm, bullying, and more. You do not need to be in a life-threatening situation to reach out.

## SAMHSA National Helpline

The Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration operates a national helpline at 1-800-662-4357. This service provides free, confidential referrals to local treatment facilities, support groups, and community-based organizations for individuals and families facing mental health or substance use disorders.

The SAMHSA helpline is available twenty-four hours a day, seven days a week, in both English and Spanish. If you do not have insurance or are underinsured, the helpline can refer you to state-funded treatment programs or facilities that charge on a sliding scale based on your ability to pay.

## What to Expect When You Reach Out

Many people hesitate to contact a crisis line because they do not know what will happen. Understanding the process can make it easier to take that step.

When you call or text a crisis line, a trained counselor will answer. They will ask how you are doing and what prompted you to reach out. You are in control of the conversation — you can share as much or as little as you feel comfortable with. The counselor will not judge you, lecture you, or tell you what to do. Their role is to listen, help you process what you are feeling, and work with you to figure out next steps.

Crisis counselors will only involve emergency services if they believe there is an immediate risk to your life or someone else's life. In the vast majority of calls and texts, the conversation stays between you and the counselor. Everything you share is confidential.

You do not need to have a plan or be in immediate danger to contact a crisis line. Feeling overwhelmed, hopeless, or like you cannot cope is reason enough. These services exist for exactly those moments.

## Safety Planning

A safety plan is a written document you create in advance that outlines steps to follow when you feel a crisis building. Having a plan ready means you do not have to make decisions during your most difficult moments.

A basic safety plan includes the following elements:

1. **Warning signs**: What thoughts, feelings, or situations tell you a crisis may be developing? Examples include withdrawing from friends, increased substance use, feeling numb, or having thoughts of self-harm.

2. **Internal coping strategies**: Things you can do on your own to manage distress, such as breathing exercises, going for a walk, taking a cold shower, or journaling.

3. **People and places that provide distraction**: Friends, family members, or public places like a coffee shop or park where you can go to interrupt the crisis.

4. **People you can ask for help**: Specific individuals — a friend, family member, therapist, or mentor — along with their phone numbers.

5. **Professional resources**: Crisis line numbers (988, Crisis Text Line 741741), your therapist's contact information, and the nearest emergency room address.

6. **Making the environment safe**: Steps to reduce access to means during a crisis, such as asking someone to hold medications or staying with a friend.

Review your safety plan regularly and share it with someone you trust so they can help you follow it when needed.
