# Cognitive Behavioral Therapy Basics

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy, commonly known as CBT, is one of the most widely studied and effective forms of psychotherapy. It is based on the idea that our thoughts, feelings, and behaviors are interconnected, and that changing unhelpful thinking patterns can lead to changes in how we feel and act. CBT is used to treat a wide range of conditions including depression, anxiety disorders, post-traumatic stress, and chronic pain.

## The CBT Triangle

The foundation of CBT is the cognitive triangle, which illustrates the relationship between three elements:

- **Thoughts**: The interpretations and beliefs we have about a situation
- **Feelings**: The emotions that arise from those thoughts
- **Behaviors**: The actions we take in response to those feelings

These three elements influence each other in a continuous cycle. For example, if you think "I'm going to fail this presentation" (thought), you might feel anxious and nauseous (feeling), which could lead you to avoid preparing or call in sick (behavior). The avoidance then reinforces the belief that you cannot handle presentations, which strengthens the original negative thought.

CBT works by identifying where in this triangle you can make a change. Often, the most accessible point of intervention is at the thought level, because thoughts can be examined and tested against evidence.

## Common Cognitive Distortions

Cognitive distortions are habitual ways of thinking that are inaccurate and tend to reinforce negative emotions. Recognizing these patterns is the first step toward changing them. Here are the most common ones:

**All-or-Nothing Thinking**: Seeing things in black and white with no middle ground. If a situation is not perfect, it is a total failure. For instance, getting one question wrong on a test and concluding "I'm terrible at this subject."

**Catastrophizing**: Assuming the worst possible outcome will happen. A minor mistake at work becomes "I'm going to get fired and never find another job." This distortion often involves both magnifying the negative and minimizing the positive.

**Mind Reading**: Assuming you know what others are thinking, usually something negative about you. "Everyone at the party thought I was boring" without any actual evidence to support that conclusion.

**Fortune Telling**: Predicting negative outcomes before they happen. "There's no point in applying because I won't get the job." This creates a self-fulfilling prophecy because the prediction discourages the effort needed to succeed.

**Emotional Reasoning**: Believing that because you feel something, it must be true. "I feel like a burden to everyone, so I must be." Emotions are real, but they are not always accurate reflections of reality.

**Should Statements**: Placing rigid expectations on yourself or others using words like should, must, or ought to. "I should be further along in my career by now." These statements create guilt and frustration.

**Personalization**: Taking responsibility for events outside your control. A friend cancels plans and you think "It must be because they don't like me" rather than considering they may simply be busy.

**Overgeneralization**: Drawing broad conclusions from a single event. One rejection becomes "Nobody will ever want to date me." Watch for words like always, never, everyone, and no one.

**Discounting the Positive**: Dismissing good things that happen as flukes or exceptions. Receiving a compliment and thinking "They're just being polite" rather than accepting it as genuine.

**Labeling**: Attaching a fixed label to yourself based on one event. Instead of "I made a mistake," you conclude "I'm a failure." Labels are rigid and leave no room for nuance or growth.

## Thought Records

A thought record is a structured tool for examining and challenging cognitive distortions. When you notice a strong negative emotion, write down the following:

1. **Situation**: What happened? Describe the event factually.
2. **Automatic Thought**: What went through your mind? Write the exact thought.
3. **Emotion**: What did you feel? Rate the intensity from zero to one hundred.
4. **Evidence For**: What facts support this thought?
5. **Evidence Against**: What facts contradict this thought?
6. **Balanced Thought**: Based on all evidence, what is a more realistic way to view this situation?
7. **Outcome**: How do you feel after considering the balanced thought? Re-rate the emotion.

The goal is not to replace negative thoughts with artificially positive ones. The goal is accuracy. A balanced thought might still acknowledge difficulty while removing the distortion. "I struggled with this presentation, but I prepared well and most of it went fine" is balanced. "The presentation was amazing and everyone loved it" is not — that would be the opposite distortion.

## Practicing CBT Skills

CBT skills improve with consistent practice. Start by noticing your automatic thoughts throughout the day, especially during moments of strong emotion. You do not need to challenge every thought — simply noticing patterns is valuable. Over time, you will begin to recognize your most frequent distortions and develop faster, more automatic balanced responses.

These techniques are most effective when used alongside professional support from a licensed therapist, but the principles of identifying and examining thoughts can benefit anyone.
